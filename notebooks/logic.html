Logic#

Introduction#

Logic is the systematic study of reasoning and argumentation. It provides a structured framework for understanding how propositions (statements) relate to each other and how conclusions can be drawn from given premises. Logic is an essential tool in various disciplines, including philosophy, mathematics, computer science, linguistics, and more. It helps us evaluate the validity of arguments and make informed decisions based on sound reasoning.

Logic is governed by various laws and principles that help us analyze and manipulate propositions. For instance the Law of Non-Contradiction states that

A proposition cannot be both true and false at the same time.

Logic can be done in natural language (English, Finnish…) or through a formal language, as seen below.

Basic concepts of logic#

Propositions

  • Closed propositions are statements that can be true (T) or false (F), but not both simultaneously. For example, “The sky is blue” and “\(2 + 2 = 5\)” are closed propositions and they can be determined definitively based on established facts or logical reasoning.

  • Open propositions are statements that cannot be definitively assigned a truth value because it contains variables, ambiguous terms, or is dependent on future events or circumstances. For example “It will rain tomorrow.” and “\(5x+4=4\)” are open propositions.

Logical connectives

Logical connectives are words or symbols that combine propositions to create more complex statements. Common logical connectives include

If a statement involves multiple logical connectives, the order of evaluation is

  1. negation

  2. conjunction and disjunction

  3. implication and biconditional

A tautology is a logical statement that is always true, for example \(p \lor \neg p\), “I own a bicycle OR I don’t own a bicycle.” A contradiction in the other hand is always false, for example \(p \land \neg p\), “I own a bicycle AND I don’t own a bicycle.”
